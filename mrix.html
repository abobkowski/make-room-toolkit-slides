01

The gap

Where does student–staff partnership stop — and why does it stop there?

The gap

Where SaP frameworks currently live

Students as Partners frameworks have produced genuinely powerful tools for curriculum co-design, assessment co-creation, and pedagogical partnership.

Curriculum and learning design
Assessment co-creation
Quality assurance and review
Pedagogical partnership programs

Conference stages — where knowledge is publicly disseminated and scholarly identity is performed — remain almost entirely absent from SaP frameworks. Student voice here is often absent or tokenised (Dianati & Hickman, 2023; Clancy et al., 2025).

Academic conferences are increasingly inviting students to co-present with staff — yet almost no practical guidance exists for how to do this well.

The gap

A second gap: who gets invited

Even within existing SaP practice, partnership experiences often reach only a small number of highly engaged students — rather than building co-creation capacity across a whole cohort (Bovill, 2020; Frawley, 2023).

Whole-class SaP practices are under-utilised globally, despite evidence that equity students may particularly benefit from the belonging, self-efficacy, and professional identity gains that partnership produces (Cook-Sather, 2018).

Make Room addresses both gaps: it starts with whole-class practice, then extends that mindset into the conference stage.

The question we started with:

If we are already doing the work of co-creation inside the classroom — why are we not taking students with us when we leave it?

02

The practice

What whole-class partnership looks like before the conference invitation is ever made

The practice

Building the mindset first

In undergraduate management units at the University of Sydney Business School, whole-class partnership practices are being developed to build a co-creation mindset before any conference invitation is made.

Co-creating learning outcomes and agendas each session
Co-setting classroom norms and co-facilitating discussions
Learning by teaching — student-led content delivery

The logic: if co-creation is already normal inside the unit, the conference invitation is a natural extension — not a sudden shift in power dynamic.

Students are then transparently invited, on a genuine opt-in basis, to co-present post-course — including former students after graduation.

01

Invitation script

Transparent, opt-in language for inviting students to co-present, including former students after course completion. Adapt the bracketed sections to your context, conference, and relationship with the student.

Email / message template

"Hi [Name], I'm presenting at [conference/event] on [date] on [topic area]. Given your work in [specific area or project], I'd like to invite you to co-present with me if that's something you'd be interested in exploring."

"This is a genuine invitation and completely optional. We would design the session together from the start, and there's no expectation to take on more than you're comfortable with. I would make sure you receive [recognition: formal acknowledgement / co-authorship / CV reference / other]."

"Would you be open to a short conversation to hear more? No pressure either way."

Send as a direct personal message. Reference something specific to the student's work — generic invitations signal tokenism.

What makes an effective invitation?

P
Personal and specificReference the student's actual contribution. Generic invitations signal tokenism.
O
Opt-in, not opt-outMake refusal easy and consequence-free. Power dynamics mean students may feel obliged to say yes.
R
Recognition upfrontState what credit the student will receive before they agree. Remove ambiguity.
T
Time and scope clearBe transparent about time commitment and what co-design will involve from the outset.

Former students: Inviting alumni preserves the value of the partnership beyond the course. They bring a distinctive dual perspective — student and emerging professional — that enriches conference dialogue.

02

Role menu

A clear menu of flexible roles allows students to contribute in ways that match their skills, confidence, and career goals. Roles are not ranked; each is equally valued. Students may select a single role or combine elements.

Presenting
Co-presenter
Takes the stage to deliver content, answer questions, and represent the research alongside the academic partner.
Co-designs and presents 1+ slides or section
Participates in Q&A dialogue
Shares personal perspective or experience
Facilitating
Co-facilitator
Facilitates discussion or audience interaction before, during, or after the presentation without necessarily presenting content.
Designs and runs audience activities or polls
Manages questions from the floor
Guides small-group breakout discussion
Designing
Content co-designer
Works with the academic partner to co-design the content, narrative, and slides. May or may not present on the day.
Co-writes key sections of the presentation
Designs visual or narrative elements
Provides critical feedback on drafts
Provocation
Student provocateur
Opens or closes the session with a brief student provocation — a short, distinctive statement of student perspective or challenge to the audience.
Prepares a 3–5 minute provocation or reflection
Offers a student lens on the research question
May be pre-recorded if preferred
Research
Research collaborator
Contributes to the underlying research or scholarship being presented — co-author, data contributor, or conceptual partner.
Named on any outputs or publications
Contributed to data, analysis, or writing
Academic authorship protocols apply
Support
Event supporter
Provides practical support before or on the day — a lighter entry point for students new to conference contexts who want to be involved without presenting.
Manages tech, timer, or logistics on the day
Observes and provides feedback post-event
Pathway to co-presenting at future events

The toolkit · Components 04 & 05

Safety and recognition

04 · Safety & access checks

A 12-item checklist across four stages: before inviting, during preparation, on the day, and after the event. Covers power dynamics, Q&A comfort boundaries, accessibility, and a clear exit option for the student.

Power dynamics named explicitly before the student agrees
Q&A boundaries agreed in advance, not improvised
Student included in post-event networking — actively

05 · Recognition guidance

Tier 1 · Minimum
Acknowledgement
Named in slides, proceedings, verbal acknowledgement, LinkedIn recommendation. Applies to all contributions.
Tier 2 · Presenting roles
Professional credential
Formal reference letter, CV confirmation, named co-presenter in program.
Tier 3 · Substantial contribution
Research co-authorship
Named co-author, CRediT statement, pathway to research roles.
The toolkit · Methodological basis

How the toolkit was developed

The toolkit was developed through collaborative reflection using Brookfield's (2017) four lenses, with a fifth lens drawing on feedback from conference participants across events and institutions.

1 · Educator reflection
2 · Student co-presenter reflection
3 · Peer and colleague feedback
4 · SaP and higher education literature
5 · Conference participant feedback ★

This is collaborative reflection — not a formal research study. It is a legitimate and appropriate form of evidence for practical toolkit development, without requiring ethics approval or systematic data collection.

The findings are reported in:
Bobkowski & Ishkova (2025)
International Journal for Students as Partners, 9(2), 284–292.

04

The evidence

What whole-class partnership and conference co-presenting produces in students

The evidence

What the approach produces

Across collaborative reflection and conference participant feedback, the approach has produced observable gains consistent with published SaP evidence:

Confidence in professional communication and Q&A (Weller et al., 2013)
Scholarly identity — students locate themselves as knowledge contributors
Collaboration skills and peer relationships beyond the unit
Graduate qualities — communication, collaboration, professional identity (Barrie, 2006)
Educator insight — richer SoTL through the student lens (Giuliano et al., 2022)

"I honestly felt like I was being handed a VIP ticket. (...) Very exhilarating. Exciting because of how new it was."

— Hannah Nguyen, student partner, in Bobkowski & Ishkova (2025, p. 287)

That shift — from student to public co-contributor of knowledge — is what this toolkit is designed to make possible, systematically and at scale.
